Raft/Mat Foundation Design for Laval’s Complex Glacial Soils

Together, we solve the challenges of tomorrow.

DETAILS →

Setting up a total station and watching the drill rig track across a Laval site, you quickly understand why the foundation approach here needs to be different. The stratigraphy across Île Jésus, where Laval sits, is rarely straightforward—you might find a dense till layer at 4 m in one corner of the lot and 12 m of sensitive silty clay in another. Raft/mat foundation design becomes a practical solution when differential settlement is the primary concern, spreading structural loads across a continuous reinforced slab so the building moves as a single unit. The field crew typically logs the first few meters with test pits to expose the crust before deeper sampling, because the weathered top layer often hides the real behavior of the Champlain Sea deposits underneath. We combine those observations with lab consolidation curves and site-specific seismic demands from NBCC 2020 to size the mat thickness, reinforcement layout, and sub-base preparation in a way that fits the real ground conditions, not just a textbook assumption.

In Laval’s sensitive marine clay, a raft foundation isn’t just about bearing capacity—it’s about controlling differential settlement so the structure settles as a monolithic block.

Method and coverage

Laval’s geotechnical personality is shaped largely by the Champlain Sea clay—a post-glacial marine deposit that can lose significant strength when remolded. The sensitive nature of this clay means that bearing capacity is rarely the limiting factor; instead, total and differential settlement control the design. A typical raft/mat foundation in the Sainte-Dorothée or Chomedey sectors often interacts with a desiccated crust of 1.5 to 3 m thickness, underlain by soft to firm silty clay extending to refusal on till or rock. The modulus of subgrade reaction derived from plate load tests or back-calculated from CPTu data tends to be highly variable, so we avoid generic values and run multiple iterations with different soil-structure interaction models. Reinforcement in the mat is detailed not only for flexure under column loads but also to resist the shrinkage and temperature effects that Quebec’s freeze-thaw cycles impose on exposed slabs. Where the clay is thicker, the design often incorporates a rigid inclusion or stone column support system beneath the raft edge to smooth the stiffness transition between the building footprint and the surrounding grade, reducing the long-term rotation that can affect elevator pits and underground parking ramps.

Regional considerations

One thing we see repeatedly in Laval is that projects on shallow mat foundations without a proper under-slab drainage system develop long-term tilting or cracking within the first five to ten years. The mechanism is almost always tied to water—either a perched groundwater table within the granular fill that softens the underlying clay, or differential heave from frost penetration under unheated slab areas. The Champlain Sea clay can lose up to 80% of its undisturbed strength when disturbed, so any excavation instability or pumping from an open sump during construction can create soft zones that the mat will settle into later. Even a well-designed raft/mat foundation can perform poorly if the contractor backfills utility trenches alongside the mat with uncompacted material, creating a stiff island surrounded by loose soil. Our design reports for Laval sites now include specific notes on subgrade protection, dewatering cut-off timing, and compaction verification with sand cone tests, because these construction-phase details make the difference between a mat that performs for fifty years and one that starts tilting before the landscaping is finished.

Other technical services

01

Geotechnical Investigation for Raft Design

We execute a targeted field program combining CPTu soundings, thin-wall Shelby tube sampling, and geophysical MASW lines to map the depth to till and characterize the sensitivity of the clay. The investigation is designed per ASTM D1586 and D5778 to provide the consolidation and strength parameters required for a reliable mat foundation model.

02

Soil-Structure Interaction and Structural Design

Using the modulus of subgrade reaction derived from site-specific data, we model the mat in finite element software to capture column punching shear, flexural moments, and thermal effects. Reinforcement detailing follows CSA A23.3, and we coordinate closely with the structural engineer of record to ensure the mat integrates with shear walls and elevator cores.

03

Construction-Phase Subgrade Inspection

We provide field review during excavation and subgrade preparation to verify that the exposed soil matches the design profile. This includes proof rolling observation, compaction testing on granular fill, and confirmation that the dewatering system is functioning before the mud slab is placed.

Quick answers

What makes raft/mat foundation design different in Laval compared to other regions of Quebec?

The main difference is the widespread presence of sensitive Champlain Sea clay across Île Jésus. This marine silt and clay can lose a large portion of its undisturbed strength when disturbed, so designs must be conservative with respect to differential settlement and construction-phase disturbance. The variable depth to glacial till across Laval also means each site requires a thorough investigation—you cannot safely extrapolate soil conditions from neighboring properties.

How long does the design process take for a typical mat foundation in Laval?

From the moment the field investigation is complete, the geotechnical analysis and structural design for a typical commercial or multi-residential mat foundation in Laval usually takes three to four weeks. This includes consolidation settlement analysis, subgrade reaction modulus calibration, and coordination with the structural team. Complex projects with irregular column grids or deep soft zones may require additional iteration time.

What does a raft/mat foundation design service cost for a Laval project?

The cost for geotechnical investigation and raft/mat foundation design for a Laval site typically ranges from CA$1,530 to CA$5,430, depending on the building footprint, number of CPTu soundings and boreholes required, and the complexity of the settlement analysis. A site-specific proposal is prepared after an initial review of the architectural loads and previous geotechnical data for the area.

Can a raft/mat foundation be designed without deep boreholes if we have CPTu data?

CPTu data is excellent for profiling stratigraphy and estimating undrained shear strength continuously, but for a definitive raft/mat foundation design in Laval we still recommend at least one borehole with thin-wall Shelby tube sampling. Consolidation parameters from oedometer tests on undisturbed samples are essential for calculating the magnitude and time rate of settlement under the mat's sustained loads.
